# 🏢 German Work Culture Guide

> **The unwritten rules nobody tells you about until it's too late.**

## 🕐 Working Hours & Time Culture

Germans take punctuality seriously — and working hours too.

**Standard working hours** are 38-40 hours/week (often 8:00-17:00 or 9:00-18:00). Many companies offer **Gleitzeit** (flexitime), meaning you have core hours (e.g., 10:00-15:00) and can flex around them.

**Overtime (Überstunden):** Check your contract carefully. Many contracts include the clause "Überstunden sind mit dem Gehalt abgegolten" — meaning overtime is included in your salary. In tech, this is common. If your contract says this, you won't get paid extra for overtime, but German labor law still caps working hours at 10h/day (averaged over 6 months).

**The key cultural difference:** Germans work hard *during* work hours and then stop. Staying late is NOT a badge of honor — it signals you can't manage your time. Leaving at 17:00 sharp is completely normal and expected.

## 📅 Vacation & Holidays

Germany is generous with time off.

**Legal minimum:** 20 vacation days/year (based on 5-day week). Most companies offer **28-30 days**.

**Public holidays:** 9-13 days depending on your Bundesland (state). Bavaria and Baden-Württemberg have the most (12-13). Berlin has the fewest (9-10). These are ON TOP of your vacation days.

**Cultural norms around vacation:**
- Taking 2-3 weeks off in a row is normal and expected. Nobody will judge you.
- "Brückentage" (bridge days) — when a holiday falls on Thursday, many people take Friday off to create a 4-day weekend. Plan early; these days book fast.
- Unused vacation days usually expire on March 31 of the following year (unless your contract says otherwise).
- Your employer cannot reject your vacation request without a good reason.

## 📋 The Probezeit (Probation Period)

Almost every German employment contract includes a **6-month probation period**.

During Probezeit:
- Either side can terminate with only **2 weeks' notice** (vs. 1-3 months after)
- You still earn full salary and benefits
- You can take vacation (it accrues from day 1)
- Performance expectations should be clearly communicated

**Survival tip:** In the first 6 months, be reliable, punctual, and proactive about asking for feedback. Don't assume "no news is good news" — ask your manager directly how you're doing at the 3-month mark.

## 📜 The Kündigungsfrist (Notice Period)

After probation, notice periods in Germany are long. The legal minimum is:

| Time with employer | Notice period |
|-------------------|---------------|
| 0-6 months (Probezeit) | 2 weeks |
| 6 months - 2 years | 4 weeks (to 15th or end of month) |
| 2-5 years | 1 month (to end of month) |
| 5-8 years | 2 months |
| 8-10 years | 3 months |
| 10+ years | 4+ months |

**Important:** Many tech companies set a standard **3-month notice period** from day 1 in the contract. This is legal and very common. New employers know this and will wait.

**Tip for job seekers:** If you're interviewing, mention your notice period early. German companies expect to wait 2-3 months for candidates.

## 📄 The Arbeitszeugnis (Employment Reference)

When you leave a job in Germany, your employer is **legally required** to give you an Arbeitszeugnis — a formal reference letter.

**The catch:** German employment references use coded language. Everything sounds positive, but there's a hidden grading system.

| What it says | What it actually means |
|-------------|----------------------|
| "stets zu unserer vollsten Zufriedenheit" (always to our fullest satisfaction) | Excellent (Grade 1) |
| "stets zu unserer vollen Zufriedenheit" (always to our full satisfaction) | Very good (Grade 2) |
| "zu unserer vollen Zufriedenheit" (to our full satisfaction) | Good (Grade 3) |
| "zu unserer Zufriedenheit" (to our satisfaction) | Adequate (Grade 4) |
| "hat sich bemüht" (made an effort) | Poor — this is devastating |

**What to do:**
- Always request a "qualifiziertes Arbeitszeugnis" (detailed reference), not just a simple confirmation of employment
- Review it carefully before accepting — you have the right to request corrections
- If you can't decode the language, there are online tools and lawyers who specialize in this

## 🏛️ The Betriebsrat (Works Council)

Many German companies with 5+ employees have a Betriebsrat — an elected employee council with real legal power.

**What they do:**
- Must be consulted before any termination (after Probezeit)
- Negotiate working conditions, overtime rules, remote work policies
- Can block unfair dismissals
- Represent employees in disputes with management

**Why it matters to you:** If your company has a Betriebsrat, you have significantly more job protection than in most other countries. Unfair dismissal is very difficult for employers.

## 💬 Communication Style

German workplace communication is different from what you might be used to.

**Direct but not rude:** Germans value clarity over diplomacy. "This doesn't work" is not an insult — it's efficient communication. Don't take directness personally.

**Formal at first:** Use "Sie" (formal you) with colleagues until explicitly invited to use "Du" (informal). In most tech companies and startups, everyone uses "Du" from day 1 — but in traditional companies, banking, law, or government, start with "Sie."

**Email culture:** German business emails tend to be structured and to the point. The typical format is greeting → context → request → closing. "Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren" (Dear Ladies and Gentlemen) for formal, "Hallo Frau/Herr [Name]" for semi-formal, "Hi [Name]" only if you're on Du terms.

**Meeting culture:** Meetings start on time (arrive 2-3 min early). Agendas are expected. Small talk is brief. Decisions made in meetings are binding.

## 🍽️ Lunch & Social Norms

- **Lunch break** is typically 30-60 minutes and often spent with colleagues in the company canteen (Kantine) or nearby restaurants
- **Frühstück** (breakfast) at the desk is common — many Germans eat bread rolls (Brötchen) at their desk in the morning
- **After-work drinks** happen but are less frequent than in the US/UK. When they happen, they're meaningful for relationship-building
- **Birthday tradition:** In Germany, the birthday person brings cake/snacks for the team (not the other way around!)
- **Kaffee und Kuchen** (coffee and cake) at ~15:00 is a cherished tradition in some offices

## 🚫 Things to Avoid

1. **Don't discuss salary openly** — German culture considers this private (ironic given this repo exists to change that)
2. **Don't work on Sundays** — it's legally restricted and culturally sacred rest time
3. **Don't skip the Anmeldung** — register your address within 14 days of moving. Everything depends on this
4. **Don't ignore the Rundfunkbeitrag** — the €18.36/month TV/radio license fee is mandatory. Everyone pays it regardless of whether they own a TV
5. **Don't jaywalk** — Germans wait for the green light even at empty streets. You'll get disapproving looks (and occasionally fined)
