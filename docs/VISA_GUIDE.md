# 🛂 Visa & Work Permit Guide for Germany

> **A practical guide to working legally in Germany** — EU Blue Card, residence permits, job seeker visa, and surviving the Ausländerbehörde.

[← Back to main page](../README.md)

## 🗺️ Quick Decision Tree

```
Are you an EU/EEA/Swiss citizen?
├── YES → You can work freely. No visa needed. Just register at Bürgeramt.
└── NO → Continue below ↓

Do you have a job offer in Germany?
├── YES → Apply for EU Blue Card or §18b Residence Permit
└── NO → Apply for Job Seeker Visa (§20 AufenthG) — 6 months to find a job

Is your salary ≥ €45,300/year (2024 threshold)?
├── YES → EU Blue Card (best option)
└── NO → §18b Residence Permit (still possible, more restrictions)

Is your degree recognized in Germany?
├── YES (in Anabin as H+) → Straightforward process
└── NO or UNCLEAR → Get a ZAB recognition (Zeugnisbewertung) FIRST
```

## 🔵 EU Blue Card (Blaue Karte EU)

**The gold standard for skilled workers.** Most tech workers should aim for this.

### Requirements
- A recognized university degree (check [Anabin](https://anabin.kmk.org/anabin.html) — your institution needs "H+" status)
- A job offer or contract with a German employer
- Minimum salary: **€45,300/year gross** (2024, general) or **€41,042/year** for shortage occupations (IT, engineering, science, medicine)
- The job must match your qualification (roughly related field)

### Benefits
- Valid for **4 years** (or duration of contract + 3 months)
- **Permanent residence** after 27 months (or 21 months with B1 German)
- Your spouse gets an **unrestricted work permit** automatically
- You can change jobs after 12 months without Ausländerbehörde approval (notify them, but no permission needed)
- Counts toward German citizenship (8 years, or 6 with integration, or 5 with special integration + C1 German)

### Application Process

1. **Before arriving:** Apply for a national visa (Typ D) at the German embassy in your home country
   - Processing time: 4-12 weeks (varies wildly by country)
   - Documents: passport, degree + ZAB recognition, job contract, proof of health insurance, biometric photos
   
2. **After arriving in Germany:**
   - Register your address at the **Bürgeramt** within 14 days (Anmeldung)
   - Book appointment at the **Ausländerbehörde** for your Blue Card
   - Bring: passport with visa, Anmeldung confirmation, job contract, degree recognition, health insurance, biometric photo, filled application form

### Anabin Check: Is My Degree Recognized?

1. Go to [anabin.kmk.org](https://anabin.kmk.org/anabin.html)
2. Search for your university under "Institutionen"
3. Look for status: **H+** = recognized, **H+/-** = partially (needs case-by-case), **H-** = not recognized
4. If not found or H-, get a **ZAB Zeugnisbewertung** (credential evaluation):
   - Apply at [kmk.org/zab](https://www.kmk.org/zab/zeugnisbewertung.html)
   - Costs ~€200, takes 2-3 months
   - This is the official recognition and is accepted everywhere

## 📋 §18b Residence Permit (Aufenthaltserlaubnis für qualifizierte Fachkräfte)

For skilled workers who don't meet Blue Card salary thresholds.

### Requirements
- Recognized university degree OR recognized vocational qualification
- Job offer in a related field
- No minimum salary (but employer must pay "comparable" wages)
- Employer may need to get ZAV approval (Bundesagentur für Arbeit checks that no EU citizen is available)

### Key Differences from Blue Card
- Permanent residence after **4 years** (vs. 21-27 months for Blue Card)
- Job changes require Ausländerbehörde approval for the first 2 years
- Spouse work permit may have restrictions

## 🔍 Job Seeker Visa (§20 AufenthG)

**Don't have a job yet?** This visa gives you 6 months to find one in Germany.

### Requirements
- Recognized university degree
- Proof of financial means (~€11,208 in a blocked account or Verpflichtungserklärung)
- Health insurance coverage
- **You cannot work on this visa** — only attend interviews and network

### Tips
- Apply 3-6 months before your planned arrival
- Network aggressively: attend meetups, use LinkedIn/Xing, go to career fairs
- Berlin, Munich, and Hamburg have the most English-friendly job markets
- Convert to Blue Card or §18b once you have a job offer — you can do this **without leaving Germany**

## 🏛️ Surviving the Ausländerbehörde

The Ausländerbehörde (foreigners' registration office) is... an experience. Here's how to survive:

### Practical Tips
- **Book appointments ASAP** — slots can be weeks or months out. In Berlin, use the online booking system the moment slots open (usually early morning)
- **Bring everything in triplicate** — originals + copies of every document
- **Arrive early** — lines form before opening
- **Bring a German speaker** if your German isn't strong — not all officers speak English
- **Be polite but persistent** — if you get conflicting information, ask to speak with a supervisor
- **Keep certified translations** of all documents (sworn translator — "beglaubigte Übersetzung")

### Documents Checklist
- [ ] Valid passport (6+ months validity)
- [ ] Biometric photos (35x45mm, recent)
- [ ] Anmeldung confirmation
- [ ] Job contract (signed by both parties)
- [ ] Degree certificate + ZAB recognition or Anabin H+ proof
- [ ] Health insurance certificate (Gesetzliche or qualifying Private)
- [ ] Completed application form (from Ausländerbehörde website)
- [ ] Previous visas/permits (if applicable)
- [ ] Rental contract (Mietvertrag)

## 🏥 Health Insurance (Krankenversicherung)

**Mandatory in Germany.** You cannot get a visa or residence permit without it.

### For Employees
- If your gross salary is **≤ €69,300/year (2024)**: you MUST join a public insurer (Gesetzliche Krankenversicherung — GKV). Cost: ~14.6% of salary, split 50/50 with employer.
- If above that threshold: you can CHOOSE public or private. **Most expats should stay public** — private sounds cheaper when young but gets expensive with age and family.

### Good Public Insurers for Expats
- **TK (Techniker Krankenkasse)** — excellent English support, great app
- **AOK** — large network, good for families
- **Barmer** — good English services
